--- abstract: "

The goal of this lesson is to introduce the concepts of the normal curve, skewness and the standard deviation. The controversy over the 1994 book is also examined.

  • Look at the normal distribution without discussing the bell curve controversy. This saves time and avoids controversial material.
  • Relate the histograms produced by the normal and skew distributions to the idea of finding area under a curve by counting the rectangles that fit under it (simple graphical numerical integration). Use the grid and the scale on the normal distribution to find the area under the normal curve. (It should be close to 1).

Have students review key terms and concepts related to this lesson including:

  • Area of a rectangle
  • Various representations of data
  • Mean, median, mode, and range

" - heading: "Objectives" content: "

Let students know what they will be learning and doing today. Say something like this:

  • Today we are going to be looking at one specific way that mathematicians and statisticians look at data: the bell curve. We will be examining what this representation is and the controversy behind it.
  • We will be using the computer and Internet later, but for now I want you to keep the computer closed.

" - heading: "Teacher Input" content: "

Lead a discussion on the \"Bell Curve\" controversy.

" - heading: "Guided Practice" content: "

Introduce the concept of standard deviation or spread with the normal distribution activity. Also explore the differences between individual data points, samples of various sizes, and expected theoretical distributions.

" - heading: "Independent Practice" content: "

Revisit \"Bell Curve\" with a discussion.

Revisit the ideas of mean, median, mode, and standard deviation graphically with the Skewness activity.

" - heading: "Closure" content: "

Interconnect ideas of theoretical and experimental probability with discrete-valued histograms and continuous distributions.
